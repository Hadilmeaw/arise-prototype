# References Catalogue

# Part 1: Sources used in D1.1 and T1.3

## 1.1 STS phase decomposition

**[1] Schenkman M, Berger RA, Riley PO, Mann RW, Hodge WA (1990).** Whole-body movements during rising to standing from sitting. *Physical Therapy* 70(10):638-648.
*Used in T1.3 (5.1) (cited explicitly as "Schenkman model") and D1.1 (2.3). Schenkman et al. instrumented healthy adults with reflective markers and ground-reaction force plates, and decomposed STS into four phases based on the time-aligned kinematic and kinetic signals: flexion-momentum, momentum-transfer, extension, and stabilisation. Our six-phase decomposition extends Schenkman's four phases by separating out the post-standing balance settling (our Stabilisation) as a distinct phase and adding the descent phase, because the failure mode "patient reaches standing but the trunk is still leaned" is distinct from "patient never reaches standing".*

**[2] Roebroeck ME, Doorenbosch CAM, Harlaar J, Jacobs R, Lankhorst GJ (1994).** Biomechanics and muscular activity during sit-to-stand transfer. *Clinical Biomechanics* 9:235-244.
*Used in D1.1 (2.3) and T1.3 (5.1). Roebroeck et al. recorded simultaneous angular kinematics and the surface EMG activity of the quadriceps, hamstrings, tibialis anterior, and gastrocnemius across the four classical STS phases. The combined kinematic-plus-EMG data establishes the timing and amplitude of muscle activation expected in each phase: which muscle should fire when, and at what level.*

**[3] Janssen WGM, Bussmann HBJ, Stam HJ (2002).** Determinants of the sit-to-stand movement: a review. *Physical Therapy* 82(9):866-879.
*Used in D1.1 (2.3) as the umbrella review behind the phase model. Janssen et al. systematically reviewed 39 STS biomechanics studies published between 1979 and 2000, including the Schenkman phase model and several alternatives (Kralj's 5-phase model and the Mansfield-Maki 3-phase model). The review documents the determinants of STS performance: chair height, seat depth, foot placement, arm use, trunk strategy, age, sex, body mass. These determinants are what justify the standardised set-up requirements we put in T1.3 (11.2).*

**[4] Yoshioka S, Nagano A, Hay DC, Fukashiro S (2009).** Biomechanical analysis of the relation between movement time and joint moment development during a sit-to-stand task. *BioMed Engineering OnLine* 8:27.
*Used in D1.1 (2.3) (phase-boundary detection) and (3.3) (peak hip vertical velocity for E8): the 0.06 × body-height per-frame velocity threshold for E8 (failed stand) corresponds to the lower end of Yoshioka's reported velocity range for completed transitions.*

**[11] Powers CM (2010).** The influence of abnormal hip mechanics on knee injury: a biomechanical perspective. *JOSPT* 40(2):42-51.

**[13] Bourke AK, O'Brien JV, Lyons GM (2007).** Evaluation of a threshold-based tri-axial accelerometer fall detection algorithm. *Gait & Posture* 26:194-199.

## 1.3 Muscle and pathology associations (D1.1 §5)

**[15] Adkin AL, Frank JS, Carpenter MG, Peysar GW (2002).** Fear of falling modifies anticipatory postural control. *Experimental Brain Research* 143:160-170.
*Used in (5) to justify the inclusion of "fear of falling" in the clinical pattern attributed to E2 (insufficient forward lean).*

**[16] Bohannon RW (2007).** Knee extension strength and body weight determine sit-to-stand independence after stroke. *Physiotherapy Theory and Practice* 23:291-297.
*Used in (5) (E3 muscle association: quadriceps strength as STS prerequisite, stroke context). This is the decision support for our E3 attribution of quadriceps weakness and is also the basis for why the per-patient personalisation layer in D1.1 (6.7) requires the patient to have completed at least five clinically correct repetitions before per-patient scoring activates: weaker patients may not produce enough correct reps to anchor the baseline.*

**[17] Mizner RL, Snyder-Mackler L (2005).** Altered loading during walking and sit-to-stand is affected by quadriceps weakness after total knee arthroplasty. *Journal of Orthopaedic Research* 23:1083-1090.
*Used in (5) (E4 muscle association: post-TKA asymmetric loading). This is the basis for our attribution of E4 (asymmetric knee extension) to the post-arthroplasty pattern, and for the inference that detecting E4 in a post-TKA patient flags a need for unilateral quadriceps strengthening.*

**[18] Maki BE, McIlroy WE (1997).** The role of limb movements in maintaining upright stance: the "change-in-support" strategy. *Physical Therapy* 77:488-507.
*Used in (5) (E5 trunk sway pathology context: balance compensation), which is what justifies treating excessive lateral trunk motion during STS as a clinical signal (E5) rather than as benign variability.*

**[19] Cesari M, Cerullo F, Zamboni V, Di Niro V, Onder G, Liperoti R, et al. (2009).** Added value of physical performance measures in predicting adverse health-related events. *Journal of the American Geriatrics Society* 57:251-259.
*Used in (5) (E6 slow movement, frailty marker), that justifies flagging slow STS to the therapist as a meaningful longitudinal signal.*

**[20] Mak MK, Levin O, Mizrahi J, Hui-Chan CWY (2003).** Joint torques during sit-to-stand in healthy subjects and people with Parkinson's disease. *Clinical Biomechanics* 18:197-206.
*Used in (5) (E6b knee instability, Parkinson context). The paper is the basis for using lateral knee oscillation and hip-velocity as detection signals for E6b, and for attributing E6b to Parkinson disease as a pattern.*

**[21] Cheng PT, Liaw MY, Wong MK, Tang FT, Lee MY, Lin PS (1998).** The sit-to-stand movement in stroke patients and its correlation with falling. *Archives of Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation* 79:1043-1046.
*Used in D1.1 (3.4) (shoulder-height differential threshold for E7) and (5) (E7 pathology association: hemiparesis). The 0.075 × body height threshold for E7 and the source of the E7 attribution to post-stroke hemiparesis.*

**[22] LaStayo PC, Woolf JM, Lewek MD, Snyder-Mackler L, Reich T, Lindstedt SL (2003).** Eccentric muscle contractions: their contribution to injury, prevention, rehabilitation, and sport. *JOSPT* 33:557-571.
*Used in (5) (E9 uncontrolled descent: eccentric quadriceps weakness).*

**[23] Alexander NB, Schultz AB, Warwick DN (1991).** Rising from a chair: effects of age and functional ability on performance biomechanics. *Journal of Gerontology* 46:M91-M98.
*Used in (5) (E11 hands-assist: chair-push and knee-push patterns).*

**[24] Hughes MA, Weiner DK, Schenkman ML, Long RM, Studenski SA (1994).** Chair rise strategies in the elderly. *Clinical Biomechanics* 9:187-192.
*Used in (5) (E11 hands-assist, companion to [23]).*

**[25] Hughes MA, Schenkman ML (1996).** Chair rise strategy in the functionally impaired elderly. *Journal of Rehabilitation Research and Development* 33:409-412.
*Used in D1.1 (3.3) (hip-velocity zero-crossings for E6b, stalled-ascent phenomenon).*

## 1.4 STS clinical tests (5xSTS, 30sCST, TUG, SPPB)

**[26] Csuka M, McCarty DJ (1985).** Simple method for measurement of lower extremity muscle strength. *American Journal of Medicine* 78(1):77-81.
*Used in T1.3 (4.3) and D1.1 (1). The original paper that introduced the Five Times Sit-to-Stand test as a simple, equipment-free measure of lower-extremity muscle function.*

**[27] Bohannon RW (2011).** Test-retest reliability of the five-repetition sit-to-stand test: a systematic review. *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research* 25(11):3205-3207.
*Used in T1.3 (4.3) (5xSTS reliability data).*

**[28] Bohannon RW (2006).** Reference values for the five-repetition sit-to-stand test: a descriptive meta-analysis. *Perceptual and Motor Skills* 103:215-222.
*Used in D1.1 (3.6) (lift-off > 3 s threshold for E6 slow movement).*

**[29] Jones CJ, Rikli RE, Beam WC (1999).** A 30-s chair-stand test as a measure of lower body strength in community-residing older adults. *Research Quarterly for Exercise and Sport* 70(2):113-119.
*Used in T1.3 (4.3) and D1.1 (1).*

## 1.5 STS as a predictor of outcomes (T1.3 §4.1)

**[30] Guralnik JM, Ferrucci L, Pieper CF, Leveille SG, Markides KS, Ostir GV, Studenski S, Berkman LF, Wallace RB (2000).** Lower extremity function and subsequent disability. *J Gerontol A* 55:M221-M231.
*Used in T1.3 (4.1) (STS as a disability predictor). This is the source for the T1.3 (4.1) claim that STS performance predicts loss of independence.*

**[31] Tinetti ME, Speechley M, Ginter SF (1988).** Risk factors for falls among elderly persons living in the community. *N Engl J Med* 319(26):1701-1707.
*Used in T1.3 (4.1) (STS as a fall predictor). This is the foundational citation for treating STS performance as a fall-risk indicator and underpins the entire fall-risk framing of the value proposition.*

**[32] Buatois S, Perret-Guillaume C, Gueguen R, Miget P, Vançon G, Perrin P, Benetos A (2008).** A simple clinical scale to stratify risk of recurrent falls in community-dwelling adults aged 65 years and older. *Physical Therapy* 88(12):1482-1491.
*Used in T1.3 (4.1) (STS-based fall stratification scale). The paper is the modern operational source for the 15-second 5xSTS threshold and feeds into the Innovina interpretation of the per-lift-off-3-second threshold for E6.*
